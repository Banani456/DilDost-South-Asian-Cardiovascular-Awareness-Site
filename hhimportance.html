Why is South Asian Cardiovascular Health so Important?

Medical Racism and The Cardiovascular Health of South Asian Americans

 ‘Masaledaar’ in Hindi translates to ‘full of masala’ or ‘flavorful’ often in reference to food. Unfortunately, this innate cultural additive is often dubbed the poison of the South Asian diet and blamed for various cardiovascular diseases in the community. However, doctors have only been feeding us half-truths. 10 years ago, a study ironically dubbed MASALA was launched in efforts to study long-term heart health in SA Americans, and the findings have been kept distastefully quiet. A handful of genetic deviations, cultural, socioeconomic factors, and a dash of insulin resistance paired with an array of other reasons make a South Asian body more susceptible to cardiovascular diseases. All of these incrementing factors make an overwhelming difference in how they should receive cardiovascular care. A caucasian-centric recipe for disease treatment has been ingrained in South Asians for decades and this ineffective one-size-fits-all approach to diverse bodies has jeopardized and taken the lives of many. By examining the history and context of medical racism, its impact on South Asian cardiovascular health, and current inclusive approaches, systemic biases within the healthcare system must be addressed to ensure equitable access to care and improve health outcomes.

 To grasp the exclusion in cardiovascular treatment, it's essential to contextualize the historical racism and medical bias against South Asian Americans. Between 1897 and 1924, the first group of SA farmers arrived in the United States. Unfortunately, SAs were “treated as undesirable immigrants … labeled enslaved, effeminate, caste-ridden and degraded.” (Bhandari 4). After anti-Asian riots and laws like the Asiatic Barred Zone Act (1917) and Oriental Exclusion Act (1924), the sparse uneducated farmers were left isolated as the only South Asian population in the US. Consequently, they couldn’t fight for their rights, let alone establish a medical plan personalized to their body. Basic survival needs had to be met first. Such treatment reflects lessons in both Bell Hooks “The Oppositional Gaze” and Carol Vance’s “Social Construction Theory”. In “The Oppositional Gaze”, a discriminative gaze brews fear when defining one’s identity and South Asians were very afraid of speaking up for their rights in such tumultuous times. On the other hand, Vance explains that one's sexuality is reliant on conformity to societal culture or lack thereof which led to gay and lesbian identities being dismissed as they were not seen as pre-existing but as social constructs. Denial of participation in studies for years is dismissive of the physiological differences between SA bodies and caucasian bodies as a social construction. Medical racism against South Asians and their cardiovascular needs began here.

 When travel bans were lifted in the 1960s, three waves of South Asians entered the states. The first wave comprised highly educated individuals, as South Asia's caste system restricted migration based on caste. While problems of discrimination still prevailed, this change in social class altered the national perception of SAs. These migrants painted South Asians as the ‘model minority’- a historic exclusion of SAs stemming from being perceived as being socioeconomically strong and consequently assumed to be well-off in all aspects. “Traditionally, research efforts to address health inequities only extend to those minority groups defined as underserved [leaving] most Asian and Asian American populations … entirely excluded.” (Shen 2021). Such exclusive practices are a perfect example of race being a social construct as seen in Dorothy Robert’s “The Invention of Race”. The model minority myth is a reflection of stereotypes constructed by race while simultaneously taking away access to healthcare for South Asians. Such assumptions led to most researchers never adapting their studies for SA bodies. While South Asians may hold high-paying jobs, this does not mean the minority should be considered less deserving when it comes to healthcares catering to them.

 The second wave began in the 1980s “continued to be dominated by students and working professionals but also included large numbers of immigrants who were family members of those who had entered the United States in the 1960s and 1970s” (Bhandari 7). This population was affected most by the colonial famines and “it was reported that coronary heart disease mortality was expected to increase approximately 29 percent in women and 48 percent in men in developed countries between 1990 and 2020” (Krishnan 2019). With the coming of the third wave of migrants in the post-2000’s who meet the latter statistics, this urgent need was met with the birth of the MASALA study through Alka Kanaya and Namratha Kandula. Their work is an example of the rebellious gaze of defiance discussed in Hooks text. Such phenomena align with B.A. Davenport's "Witnessing and the Medical Gaze," where medical professionals are cautioned against fixating on patients' socioeconomic status, race, or gender. However, the need to adapt studies to different bodies to save lives is equally as important and the lessons in this text should be taken as advice to save every patient by adapting to their needs instead of giving biased and unhelpful medical treatment due to a patients’ background. Until the SA population was strong enough to sustain itself, the healthcare system in the United States turned a blind eye investigating the South Asian cardiovascular public health crisis.

 MASALA’s first research subjects were composed of post-1965 immigrants with bodies largely differing from the first migrants. Still reeling from colonialism famines, “South Asians have a tendency to generate and store fat and not burn it off, amassing low lean muscle mass … because South Asians are “starvation-adapted”, due to having to survive at least 31 famines” (Bakar 2022). Research at Brown University shows that one famine survivor doubles the risk of diabetes and obesity in the next generation and raises cardiovascular disease risk 2.7 times for the following two. Not accounting for these variables leads to issues like heart disease calculators failing to accurately compute the risk for SA patients, jeopardizing their treatment and recovery (AHA 2021). The post-1965 immigrants meet these unfortunate statistics, which MASALA accounts for in their research. South Asians also have various cultural reasons to receive personalized target care for heart diseases. A traditional SA diet is associated with deteriorating cardiovascular health. Various “traditional snacks, sweets, and entrees are rich in refined carbohydrates, are fried, and contain a high content of saturated fat.” (Shah et. al.) with even cooking methods reducing nutritional value of meals. Additionally, tobacco - a known contributor to cardiovascular diseases - and traditional forms like ‘paan’ (betel leaves) are often not accounted for in their consumption. When implementing change, it is easier to begin at a familiar level. For example, advising a religiously synonymous vegetarian diet, that holds lower resistance to insulin and lower high-density lipoprotein cholesterol, is a much healthier alternative than a western based diet. Furthermore, “dietary choices (Mediterranean diet or a diet high in fruits, vegetables, nuts and legumes) and social connectedness' ' (Shah et. al. 2022) are associated with improved cardiovascular health.  Culturally in tune, group exercise is more effective for South Asians contrasting with the traditional lack of emphasis on exercise in SA lifestyles. The pursuit of newfound methods for recovery for South Asian parallels with the journey of cancer perception, treatment and prevention in the context of race in Keith Wailoo’s “White Plague: How Cancer Crossed the Color Line”. As research broadened beyond its focus on upper-class white women, solutions for diverse bodies emerged, mirroring current efforts tailored to SAs.

 MASALA’s research is revolutionizing healthcare for South Asians but the American Healthcare system must support it. A new development extending to the future is Congresswoman Pramila Jayapal’s new legislature seeking funding for South Asian heart health promotion and ASCVD research. In light of this, medical racism cannot continue to diagnose different bodies with a narrow-minded approach. The medical community must fund, incentivize and prioritize medical research that centers diverse bodies particularly long excluded SA bodies. It is high time to start saving South Asians lives and keeping the community's heart pumping.
